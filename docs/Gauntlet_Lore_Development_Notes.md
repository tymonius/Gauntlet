# Gauntlet Lore Development Notes

**Status:** Early setting-development notes; no complete canon has been approved.  
**Scope:** Setting, tone, faction-world relationship, and rejected lore directions. This file is not rules text.

---

## 1. Development approach

Build the setting slowly.

Do not attempt to write the full history, world map, creation myth, political system, or definitive campaign premise in one pass. Establish one preference at a time, test whether it feels right, and preserve uncertainty where the user has not committed.

Lore should support the game rather than explain every rule object.

---

## 2. Current tonal direction

The material culture should feel broadly **late-medieval / early-modern / Colonial-adjacent**, with room for:

- black powder, muskets, cannon, pikes, cavalry, and swords;
- forts, roads, ships, courts, councils, banks, religious orders, laboratories, scouts, and spy networks;
- parchment, wax seals, ledgers, maps, field equipment, ritual objects, and early-modern institutions;
- old ruins, strange places, unreliable maps, and partially understood powers as background texture.

This is an aesthetic and technological reference point, not a one-to-one fantasy version of a real historical period or nation.

Mystery, frontier danger, ruins, expeditions, and rumors are acceptable, but should not become the entire premise. Gauntlet is primarily a conflict game about institutions, maneuver, rivalry, and contested ground.

Working tonal principle:

> The world should feel old, strange, and only partially understood, while the immediate story remains grounded in faction conflict.

---

## 3. Factions as institutions

The six factions represent **institutions and methods of power**, not six fixed nations, ethnic groups, or isolated cultures.

Current factions:

- Military;
- Diplomats;
- Inquisition;
- Mystics;
- Financiers;
- Intelligence.

This allows a deck or player force to represent a coalition, court, army, order, guild, expedition, office, conspiracy, or institutional bloc rather than a nation-state.

The factions should feel as though they arose from a broadly **shared civilization or cultural sphere**. They may share laws, ranks, symbols, currencies, technologies, religious language, and political traditions while competing over how power should be exercised.

---

## 4. Current working premise

A promising, but not yet final, direction is:

> The six institutions once served or participated in a larger overarching order. That central authority collapsed, fractured, disappeared, or lost legitimacy. The institutions continued operating without a trusted center and now compete to preserve, restore, redefine, or dominate the shared civilization.

Possible versions of that collapse include:

- a dead or vanished Crown;
- a fractured ruling council;
- a fallen, sealed, occupied, or inaccessible capital;
- a failed sacred, legal, or magical mandate;
- a combination such as a Broken Council following the fall of the capital.

No version has been selected yet.

The institutions should not necessarily think of themselves as villains or rebels. Each may claim that it alone can preserve order, prevent catastrophe, or restore legitimate authority.

---

## 5. The Gauntlet in the setting

Do not over-explain the Gauntlet or make it universally mystical.

A Gauntlet may simply be a narrow strategic route, campaign corridor, chain of strongpoints, pass, road, river route, causeway, or contested approach where supplies, geography, and military pressure concentrate conflict.

Different matches or scenarios may represent different Gauntlets.

The game board does not require one universal legendary prize at the far end. The conflict may concern a city, fort, road, port, mine, pass, sacred site, political center, supply route, ruin, or other strategic objective.

The unnamed positions beyond each end of the Gauntlet are rules endpoints, not defined regions of the setting. They should not automatically become sacred, metaphysical, or world-defining lore objects.

---

## 6. Rejected or deprecated directions

Do not revive these as the default premise without an explicit new decision:

### Direct Old World / New World analogue

Rejected:

- fantasy Europe discovering a fantasy America;
- explicit colonial powers arriving in an inhabited New World;
- a historical-colonial narrative mapped closely onto real-world conquest.

The early-modern aesthetic can remain without reproducing that geopolitical structure.

### Age of Claims / colonial legal fiction

Rejected as too explanatory and politically overbuilt:

- formal claim systems as the central reason Gauntlets exist;
- sanctioned colonial wars deciding legal title;
- excessive emphasis on legitimacy, charters, and territorial claims as the world premise.

Those ideas may appear locally where appropriate, especially for Diplomats or Financiers, but should not define the whole setting.

### Ancient-road cosmology

Rejected or set aside:

- ancient roads reconnecting distant realms or capitals;
- the Gauntlet as a sacred succession mechanism;
- a universal metaphysical explanation for every battlefield;
- one vanished empire explaining all ruins, magic, politics, and conflict.

### Universal legendary prize

Do not assume every faction is racing toward the same throne, vault, weapon, garden, god, or relic. Such an object may work in a specific scenario, but not as the mandatory setting premise.

### Mystery as the whole identity

Do not turn Gauntlet into primarily an expedition, treasure-hunt, lost-city, or exploration game. Mystery and strange terrain should enrich the conflict rather than replace it.

---

## 7. Relationship to faction art direction

The leader-design documents establish a shared early-modern / Colonial-adjacent visual language. Lore development should remain compatible with that material culture while avoiding literal historical depictions.

The institutional premise supports the visual roster:

- Military officers can belong to the same broad order as court diplomats;
- Inquisition officials can share clothing and social forms with Mystic scholars while opposing them;
- Financiers can fund armies, courts, expeditions, or rival institutions;
- Intelligence leaders can operate across all institutional boundaries.

Lore should explain coexistence and rivalry without forcing every faction into a separate culture.

---

## 8. Open setting questions

Develop one at a time:

1. What exactly collapsed: Crown, Council, Capital, Mandate, or a combination?
2. How long ago did the collapse occur?
3. Are the factions formally at war, operating through proxies, or competing inside a nominally shared government?
4. What geographic scale does a normal match represent?
5. Are Gauntlets common strategic corridors, a formal campaign tradition, or simply the game's abstraction for many kinds of contested routes?
6. How public and accepted are Mystic practices?
7. Is the Inquisition primarily religious, legal, political, or a hybrid institution?
8. How independent are chartered financial and intelligence institutions from military and civil authority?
9. What non-faction populations, local powers, towns, and communities inhabit the world?
10. How much supernatural certainty should the setting provide?

Do not answer all ten before the core premise has been tested through smaller discussions.
