# Gauntlet v0.6 Military Design Notes

**Status:** Active faction-design brief for the Military card package.  
**Purpose:** Define Military's strategic identity, overlapping design threads, intended weaknesses, and package-evaluation standard before individual cards are approved.

Use this document with:

- `Gauntlet_v0.6_Working_Rules.md` for current Military, General, Commandant, Command, and Order rules;
- `Gauntlet_v0.6_Faction_Card_Design_Guide.md` for the governing card and package standards;
- `Gauntlet_v0.6_Neutral_Card_Pool.md` for shared tools Military already has access to;
- the active card metadata and review records for inherited Military candidates.

This document does not approve any Military card. Existing candidates must still earn a place under the faction-card design guide.

---

## 1. Core identity

Military is about:

> **Turning battlefield success into strategic advantage while deciding how far to press before success becomes overextension.**

Military should not be defined merely as the faction with larger battle modifiers or more attacking cards. Its identity comes from converting victories into movement, retreat pressure, occupation, capture, positional leverage, and further operations.

Its central decision is:

> **What is the most valuable thing I can do with this victory, and how much more should I commit to get it?**

This question unifies both leaders:

- The **General** asks whether a successful operation can continue before momentum is lost.
- The **Commandant** asks whether a defended or occupied position can be converted into lasting control or a counteroffensive.

The faction's defining internal tension is **momentum versus consolidation**.

---

## 2. Strategic threads

Military cards should draw from several overlapping threads rather than form isolated leader packages or linear mechanical cycles.

### 2.1 Command discipline

Command should remain a scarce tactical resource whose best use changes with timing and board state.

Military cards may create decisions about:

- spending Command before a battle or preserving it for after the result;
- using one Command immediately or saving toward a two-Command Order;
- ending an operation after the first victory or using that victory as the start of another;
- committing to a line before knowing whether more Command will become available;
- changing the relative value of Orders without simply making them free.

Reliable passive Command generation is dangerous because it can erase the faction's core resource tension and become mandatory infrastructure.

### 2.2 Combined arms and commitment

Military strength should come from coordinating different resources and timing windows rather than stacking larger numerical bonuses.

Relevant elements may include:

- a Battle card committed from hand;
- cards played from battle draw;
- a prepared Asset;
- an Order;
- movement before the battle;
- Command reserved or spent;
- the position where the battle occurs.

The player should be rewarded for assembling an operation from several interacting pieces while risking more if that operation fails.

The desired pattern is not "play more Military cards for more bonuses." It is "coordinate resources from different sources and decide how much to commit before the result is known."

### 2.3 Maneuver and choosing the engagement

Military should care about more than whether a battle is won. It should care about:

- where the battle occurs;
- who initiated it;
- what space each player will occupy afterward;
- available retreat routes;
- whether the contested Territory is controlled, occupied, or exposed;
- whether continued movement creates another engagement;
- whether yielding ground now creates a stronger counterattack later.

Military cards should create engagement choices rather than merely repeat generic extra movement, attacker advantage, defender advantage, or retreat effects already available through Neutral cards.

Military should shape battle through visible maneuver, not through hidden Missions, negotiation, economic denial, or broad prohibitions on ordinary play.

### 2.4 Momentum versus consolidation

After winning, the Military player may want to:

- advance again;
- initiate another battle;
- pursue or displace the defeated opponent;
- capture immediately;
- draw or preserve resources;
- establish a defensible foothold;
- stop before becoming overextended.

The General naturally favors continued pressure but should sometimes benefit from consolidating. The Commandant naturally favors securing gains but should be able to convert defense into counterattack.

Cards in this thread should create a real choice between exploiting momentum and preserving position rather than simply granting both.

### 2.5 Prepared operations and force preservation

Military should be able to prepare visible major operations through Assets, timing commitments, and positional setup.

A prepared operation may ask the player to:

- establish a plan, reserve, formation, or logistical preparation;
- hold it until the correct engagement;
- sacrifice it for a larger immediate effect;
- abandon it when the board changes;
- preserve resources rather than spend everything at the first opportunity.

Prepared operations create anticipation, visible counterplay, Asset-bank pressure, and meaningful opportunity cost.

Force preservation may also reward disciplined restraint, withdrawal, unspent Command, unused battle draw, or a deliberately uncommitted resource, provided it does not become generic card recursion or unconditional draw.

## 3. Cross-thread standard

The strongest Military cards should usually touch at least two strategic threads.

Examples include:

- a prepared operation that rewards combined commitment;
- a maneuver card that creates a choice between pursuit and consolidation;
- a Command card whose optimal timing changes depending on who initiated the battle;
- a defensive preparation that can later fuel a counteroffensive;
- an offensive card whose full payoff requires preserving resources rather than spending everything immediately.

Avoid dividing the package into separate groups of Command cards, General cards, Commandant cards, movement cards, and defensive cards. Cards should connect those ideas in different combinations.

## 4. Intended weaknesses

### 4.1 Military must win through the Gauntlet

Military has no alternate victory. Its advantages must ultimately produce movement, occupation, capture, and breakthrough.

It should not build toward a disconnected endgame that avoids contested board play.

### 4.2 Command depends on battlefield success

Military gains Command through winning battles. It may experience resource drought when it cannot arrange a favorable battle, repeatedly loses, or spends Command unsuccessfully trying to secure the next victory.

Faction cards may help the player manage this weakness strategically but should not erase it through reliable passive Command generation.

### 4.3 Military should be weak at indirect control

Military should not natively excel at:

- hand surveillance;
- random discard;
- broad card cancellation;
- economic denial;
- recursion;
- negotiation;
- long-term Territory suppression;
- preventing the opponent from taking ordinary actions.

Military should answer problems primarily through position, battle, retreat, pressure, visible preparation, and operational sequencing.

### 4.4 Military should risk overextension

Its strongest plays should expose it to some combination of:

- depleted Command;
- spent Assets;
- lost hand commitments;
- poor defensive position;
- an opponent's counterattack;
- reduced flexibility if the next battle fails.

The Military player should often be tempted to push one step farther than is safe.

### 4.5 Military should not receive routine capture shortcuts

Immediate capture is exceptional. The normal occupation-and-counterattack structure should remain meaningful.

Military may have premium capture-timing effects, but the package should not make the counterattack window feel optional or duplicate the General, Commandant, and Neutral capture tools without adding a new decision.

### 4.6 Military should not automatically win battle math

Military should be tactically excellent because it chooses engagements, prepares effectively, sequences resources, and exploits outcomes—not because its cards simply contain more advantage, disadvantage, rerolls, and +1 effects than every other faction.

Routine modifier stacking is not sufficient faction identity.

### 4.7 Military should have limited generic card economy

Military may gain or preserve cards as the consequence of successful operations, disciplined restraint, consolidation, or tactical coordination.

It should not receive faction-branded unconditional draw engines, broad recursion, or endlessly replenishing resources.

---

## 5. Package evaluation standard

A completed Military package should support different mixtures of:

- rapid operational tempo;
- careful Command management;
- combined-arms battle sequencing;
- positional maneuver;
- prepared major offensives;
- defense followed by counterattack;
- force preservation;
- aggressive pursuit;
- methodical consolidation.

No single thread should dictate a solved deck, and no card should be required for Command, Orders, the General, or the Commandant to function.

Military cards should be rejected or revised when they mainly:

- add routine battle modifiers;
- provide passive Command generation;
- duplicate Neutral movement or capture effects without a new strategic choice;
- bypass the counterattack window too frequently;
- produce one obvious General package or one obvious Commandant package;
- erase overextension risk;
- turn battlefield success into automatic, consequence-free snowballing.
