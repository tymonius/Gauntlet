# Game Design Glossary

**Purpose:** A quick reference for common board game and card game terminology useful when designing, discussing, testing, writing rules for, or pitching *Gauntlet*.

This glossary uses plain-language definitions. Some terms overlap, and different publishers or players may use them slightly differently.

---

## 1. Genre and Product Terms

### Deck-building game

A game where players build or improve their decks during play.

Players usually start with a small, weak deck and acquire better cards as part of the game.

Examples: *Dominion*, *Star Realms*, *Clank!*

*Gauntlet* is not currently a deck-building game in this sense.

### Deck construction / constructed deck game

A game where players build their decks before the game begins.

Examples: *Magic: The Gathering*, *Pokemon TCG*, *Netrunner*, many expandable card games.

*Gauntlet* currently fits this category because players build decks before play.

### Customizable card game

A game where players customize decks from a larger card pool.

This is a broad term that can include trading card games, collectible card games, fixed-expansion card games, and other constructed-deck systems.

### Expandable card game

A card game designed to grow through later expansions, additional factions, additional cards, or new modes.

This does not necessarily imply randomized booster packs.

### Trading card game / TCG

A customizable card game usually sold through randomized packs, with collection and trading as part of the product model.

Example: *Magic: The Gathering*.

### Collectible card game / CCG

A term closely related to TCG. It usually emphasizes collecting randomized cards.

### Living Card Game / LCG

A fixed-expansion customizable card game model associated with Fantasy Flight Games. Mechanically, it describes a non-random expansion model, but the term itself is a branded product label.

Use caution before using this term commercially.

### Tactical card game

A game where card play creates tactical decisions about timing, conflict, positioning, or short-term advantage.

### Card-driven board game

A board game where cards drive many of the actions, events, battles, or strategic options.

### Skirmish game

A small-scale tactical conflict game, often involving units or characters fighting across a board or map.

*Gauntlet* has some skirmish-game feel, but it does not currently use units in the traditional sense.

### Lane battler

A game where conflict is structured along one or more lanes or paths.

*Gauntlet* has lane-battler qualities because players advance through a defined line of contested Territories.

### Area control / territory control

A game where players fight over spaces, regions, or areas on a board.

In some games, this means having majority presence in an area. In *Gauntlet*, territory control is more direct: a player controls, occupies, captures, or contests specific Territories.

### Asymmetric faction game

A game where different factions, characters, or decks play differently from each other.

Good asymmetry gives each faction a distinct identity while still keeping them inside the same core game.

### Engine-builder

A game or strategy where players assemble systems that generate repeated advantage over time.

Engines often produce resources, cards, actions, movement, or victory progress.

### Hand management game

A game where the central skill is deciding which cards to keep, play, commit, discard, or save.

### Bluffing game

A game where players can mislead opponents about hidden information, intentions, or strength.

### Hidden-information game

A game where some important information is known to one player but not others.

Examples include cards in hand, face-down cards, secret objectives, or unrevealed board elements.

### Alternate win condition

A way to win other than the game's default victory condition.

Alternate win conditions are strongest when they create tension and counterplay rather than bypassing the main game.

## 2. Economy Terms

### Action economy

How many meaningful things a player can do per turn.

If a player can do more useful things than the opponent, they may have an action-economy advantage.

### Card economy

How efficiently a player gains, spends, preserves, or loses cards.

Drawing cards, discarding cards, forcing the opponent to lose cards, and recovering cards all affect card economy.

### Turn economy

How many turns it takes to convert a plan into a result.

A powerful effect that takes several turns to set up may have a high turn-economy cost.

### Resource economy

How a game or faction gains, stores, spends, and converts resources.

Faction resources in *Gauntlet* include things like Command, Influence, Conviction, Capital, and Intel.

### Tempo

Immediate pressure, initiative, or momentum.

A tempo play may not create the most long-term value, but it forces the opponent to respond right now.

### Value

Long-term advantage.

Drawing extra cards, gaining repeatable income, preserving resources, or improving future options are common value plays.

### Efficiency

How much effect a player receives for a given cost.

A very efficient card or ability may be too strong even if its effect looks simple.

---

## 3. Engines and Scaling

### Engine

A repeatable system that generates ongoing advantage.

An engine might generate resources, cards, movement, battle bonuses, or victory progress.

### Ramp

A mechanic or strategy that accelerates a player's access to resources, power, or options.

Ramp often makes later turns stronger than they would normally be.

### Scaling

How a player's power increases over time.

Scaling can come from resources, board control, card engines, faction progress, or recurring income.

### Snowballing

A pattern where an early advantage helps create more advantage, which then creates even more advantage.

Snowballing is not always bad, but it becomes a problem when comebacks feel unrealistic.

### Runaway leader

A player whose lead has become self-sustaining and difficult to interrupt.

### Catch-up mechanic

A mechanic that helps a trailing player remain competitive.

Good catch-up mechanics preserve tension. Bad catch-up mechanics can feel artificial or like they punish successful play.

---

## 4. Interaction and Pressure

### Counterplay

The opponent's ability to respond meaningfully to a strategy, card, or win condition.

A powerful plan can still be fair if the opponent sees it coming and has realistic ways to interfere.

### Interaction

The degree to which players affect each other.

Battles, blocking, negotiation, disruption, and contested spaces are all forms of interaction.

### Disruption

Interfering with the opponent's plan.

Disruption may delay, weaken, redirect, reveal, cancel, or complicate what the opponent is trying to do.

### Denial

Preventing the opponent from doing something.

Denial can include blocking movement, preventing card play, removing resources, or stopping a win condition.

### Soft control

Influencing the opponent's choices without completely preventing them.

Soft control often works through incentives, pressure, costs, or negotiated outcomes.

### Hard control

Directly removing or forbidding options.

Hard control can be satisfying, but too much can create negative play experiences.

### Inevitability

A game state where one player will eventually win if the opponent does not act quickly.

Alternate victory paths often create inevitability.

---

## 5. Board-State and Positioning Terms

### Board state

Everything currently true on the table.

This includes controlled spaces, occupied spaces, revealed cards, hidden cards, resources, active effects, and any visible progress toward victory.

### Positioning

Where players are on the board and what those positions allow them to threaten or defend.

### Threat range

The spaces, cards, or objectives a player can affect from their current position.

### Reach

The ability to affect something farther away or sooner than expected.

Extra movement, long-range effects, sudden capture effects, or surprise battle effects can create reach.

### Front line

The contested area where opposing players' pressure meets.

In *Gauntlet*, this is often the Territory or Territories where occupation, counterattack, and capture pressure are concentrated.

### Choke point

A space or situation that restricts movement, concentrates conflict, or forces players to fight over a narrow path.

---

## 6. Card Play and Hidden Information

### Hand management

Choosing how to use a limited hand of cards.

This includes when to play, commit, save, discard, or sacrifice cards.

### Commitment

Putting a card, resource, or position at risk before knowing the full outcome.

Face-down battle cards are a clear example of commitment.

### Bluffing

Representing a stronger, weaker, or different play than the one you actually have.

### Telegraphing

Making your plan visible before it resolves.

Telegraphing can be good if it creates tension and counterplay, but bad if it makes the plan too easy to stop.

### Hidden information

Information known to one player but not all players.

Examples: cards in hand, face-down cards, unrevealed Territories, and secret or face-down objectives.

### Perfect information

A game state where all relevant information is visible to all players.

Chess is a classic perfect-information game. Most card games are not perfect-information games.

### Information asymmetry

A situation where one player knows something the other does not.

Information asymmetry creates bluffing, reading, deduction, and surprise.

## 7. Balance Terms

### Power level

How strong a card, faction, strategy, or mechanic is overall.

### Floor

How good something is in a bad or ordinary situation.

A high-floor card is rarely useless.

### Ceiling

How good something can be in an ideal situation.

A high-ceiling card can create big moments but may be inconsistent.

### Swinginess

How dramatically outcomes can vary because of luck, timing, or high-impact effects.

Swinginess can create excitement, but too much can make decisions feel less important.

### Variance

Randomness in outcomes.

Card draws and dice rolls are common sources of variance.

### Consistency

How reliably a deck, card, or strategy does what it is designed to do.

### Overtuned

Too strong numerically or mechanically.

### Undertuned

Too weak to compete or not rewarding enough to use.

### Degenerate strategy

A legal strategy that damages the game experience because it is repetitive, uninteractive, too reliable, too strong, or not fun to play against.

### Dominant strategy

A strategy that is usually best regardless of matchup, situation, or opponent response.

Dominant strategies reduce meaningful choice.

---

## 8. Game-Feel Terms

### Friction

Anything that slows play, creates confusion, or adds effort.

Some friction can be worth it. Too much makes a game feel clunky.

### Cognitive load

How much players must remember, track, calculate, or mentally process.

High cognitive load can make a game harder to learn and harder to enjoy.

### Bookkeeping

Mechanical tracking that is necessary but not inherently fun.

Examples include repeated calculations, many counters, frequent state checks, or complex resource updates.

### Tension

The feeling that something important is about to happen and both players can still affect the outcome.

### Pacing

How the game moves through its opening, middle, and endgame.

Good pacing keeps decisions meaningful while preventing the game from dragging.

### Downtime

Time one player spends waiting while another player makes decisions or resolves effects.

### Feel-bad

A moment that may be balanced but feels unpleasant, unfair, or deflating.

Feel-bad moments are especially important to watch in games with denial, discard, cancellation, or permanent loss.

### Splashiness

Big, dramatic, memorable effects.

Splashy moments can make a game exciting, but too many can reduce control or clarity.

---

## 9. Especially Useful Terms for Gauntlet

The most useful terms for discussing *Gauntlet* are likely:

- **Tempo** — who is applying immediate pressure?
- **Value** — who is gaining long-term advantage?
- **Action economy** — how many meaningful things can a player do this turn?
- **Card economy** — who is gaining, preserving, or losing cards?
- **Engine** — what repeatable system is creating advantage?
- **Scaling** — how does power increase as the game progresses?
- **Snowballing** — does advantage create too much more advantage?
- **Counterplay** — can the opponent respond meaningfully?
- **Interaction** — are players affecting each other or playing separately?
- **Hidden information** — what does one player know that the other does not?
- **Cognitive load** — how much must players track or remember?
- **Pacing** — is the game moving toward a satisfying conclusion?

## 10. Short Genre Description for Gauntlet

A concise technical description:

> *Gauntlet* is a constructed-deck, card-driven tactical territory duel with asymmetric factions and alternate victory paths.

A more player-facing description:

> *Gauntlet* is an expandable tactical card game where players build faction decks, battle through contested territory, capture ground, and pursue unique paths to victory.
