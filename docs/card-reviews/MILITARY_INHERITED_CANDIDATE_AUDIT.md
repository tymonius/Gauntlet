# Military Inherited Candidate Audit

**Status:** Completed design audit; recommendations await explicit approval before card registry or exact-text sources change.  
**Scope:** Brothers in Arms, Militias, Patriotism, and Shock and Awe.  
**Governing sources:** `../Gauntlet_v0.6_Faction_Card_Design_Guide.md`, `../Gauntlet_v0.6_Military_Design_Notes.md`, `../Gauntlet_v0.6_Working_Rules.md`, and `../Gauntlet_v0.6_Neutral_Card_Pool.md`.

This audit applies the no-grandfathering rule. Historical status, cost, name, and partial wording do not reserve any of these cards a place in the twelve-card Military package.

# 1. Brothers in Arms

## Current direction

Brothers in Arms rewards using both a hand-origin Battle card and a battle-drawn card in the same battle. Its prior Asset direction also preserved the hand-origin card after a victory, while its Battle direction protected the paired card from cancellation or negation.

## What works

### Clear Military behavior

The core idea expresses **combined arms and commitment**. It asks the player to coordinate resources from two different sources rather than merely play a larger modifier.

### Broad interaction surface

The concept can interact with:

- ordinary hand commitments;
- ordinary battle draw;
- cards that increase battle-draw amount;
- cards that increase battle-drawn play allowance;
- cards that protect commitments;
- both Military leaders;
- Neutral tools such as Conscription, Reinforcements, Tactical Planning, and other battle-draw support.

It therefore has the potential to support several decks instead of one named package.

### Memorable play potential

A coordinated commitment can feel like a deliberately assembled operation. The player may risk a valuable hand card because the battle draw produced the right support, or may commit early in expectation of finding a useful second component.

## What fails

### The trigger is too close to normal optimal play

Playing one card from hand and one from battle draw is already a common high-commitment battle pattern. A repeatable Asset that automatically rewards that pattern risks becoming a premium efficiency engine rather than a new strategic direction.

### The prior payoff does too many jobs

The earlier concept combined:

- advantage;
- preservation of the hand-origin card after winning;
- protection of the paired card from cancellation or negation.

Those effects improve battle strength, card economy, and reliability simultaneously. The player is not choosing among those benefits.

### Automatic-inclusion pressure

Any Military deck expecting to contest important battles could plausibly want a repeatable reward for using both normal card sources. The card could become especially difficult to omit when paired with Neutral battle-draw support.

### Limited opponent counterplay

If the paired card is protected from both cancellation and effect-negation, the opponent loses much of the normal interaction available against a heavily committed operation. Visible Asset preparation alone does not justify broad protection.

### Unclear leader differentiation

Both leaders benefit, but the existing direction does not meaningfully change between them. It reinforces committing more resources to battles without creating a General-specific momentum decision or a Commandant-specific consolidation decision.

## Rubric result

- **New decision:** Partial. The coordination requirement is promising, but the trigger may simply reward normal maximum commitment.
- **Faction behavior:** Pass at concept level.
- **Interaction breadth:** Strong.
- **Standalone floor:** Likely acceptable, depending on redesign.
- **Leader interpretation:** Weak in the current direction.
- **Mandatory-engine risk:** High for a repeatable Asset.
- **Counterplay:** Insufficient if broad protection remains.
- **Memorable potential:** Strong.
- **Obsolescence risk:** Could overshadow ordinary battle-draw support and protection cards by bundling too many benefits.
- **Meaningful tension:** Present in the seed, undercut by the accumulated rewards.

## Recommendation

**Preserve the name and combined-arms design seed, but substantially rebuild the card.**

The redesign should:

- make the player choose how or when to coordinate the two card sources;
- avoid a repeatable automatic reward for an already common play pattern;
- provide one focused payoff or a real choice among payoffs rather than strength, protection, and recovery together;
- preserve normal counterplay;
- create different valuation or sequencing for the General and Commandant;
- retain meaningful risk if the player commits heavily and loses.

Cost 4 is not protected. The eventual cost should follow the rebuilt effect.

**Release-slot status:** Unfilled until a redesigned card passes review.

# 2. Militias

## Current direction

Militias is a defensive Asset or Battle card that gives the opponent disadvantage, upgraded to double disadvantage on a Territory the Military player controls. The revised Asset direction limits the persistent trigger to the first qualifying battle each turn.

## What works

### Legible theme

Local forces defending controlled ground is understandable and thematically compatible with Military.

### Straightforward use

The card has a clear standalone floor and is easy to resolve.

## What fails

### Numerical camouflage

The design is fundamentally a defender modifier. It does not create a new decision about Command, maneuver, preparation, commitment, momentum, consolidation, or force preservation.

### Heavy overlap with Neutral cards

Neutral already supplies:

- Entrenchment;
- Fortifications;
- Palisade Wall;
- Stand Ground;
- Foothold;
- Illegal Occupation;
- Resistance;
- several other defensive or counterattack tools.

Militias distinguishes itself mainly by applying a larger disadvantage value.

### Commandant lock and solved stacking

The card naturally stacks with Entrench, Homeland Advantage, Heartland defense, defensive Battle cards, and other controlled-Territory bonuses. It strongly favors the Commandant without offering the General an interesting alternative use.

### Minimal deckbuilding creativity

A defensive Military construction would include it because it makes defensive battle math better. It does not open a new strategy; it strengthens the obvious one.

### Poor play experience at the high end

Double disadvantage can make a battle feel predetermined, especially when layered with Homeland Advantage and other defensive effects. The opponent's answer becomes “have cancellation” rather than make a positional or sequencing decision.

## Rubric result

- **New decision:** Fail.
- **Faction behavior:** Superficial pass through theme; mechanical fail.
- **Interaction breadth:** Broad but primarily additive stacking.
- **Standalone floor:** Strong, which increases automatic-inclusion pressure.
- **Leader interpretation:** Strong Commandant skew; weak General use.
- **Mandatory-engine risk:** Moderate to high in defensive decks.
- **Counterplay:** Narrow and mostly card-based.
- **Memorable potential:** Low.
- **Obsolescence risk:** Competes with Neutral defensive cards by being numerically larger.
- **Meaningful tension:** Absent.

## Recommendation

**Retire the current effect completely.**

The name or local-force theme may be reused only for a fundamentally different card that creates a decision. A future Militias design might involve visible local preparation, temporary force preservation, yielding ground, counterattack timing, or sacrificing a prepared resource, but the existing disadvantage framework should not be the starting point.

Cost 3 is not protected.

**Release-slot status:** Unfilled.

# 3. Patriotism

## Current direction

Patriotism is a controlled-Territory defensive Asset that doubles the first +1 or advantage granted by one of the Military player's Battle cards. Its Battle direction grants advantage while defending controlled Territory.

## What works

### Immediate thematic readability

The concept of stronger defense on one's own ground is easy to understand.

### Some combinatorial interaction

The Asset technically interacts with many Battle cards by modifying the first qualifying bonus.

## What fails

### Linear modifier amplification

The card does not create synergy in the desired sense. It multiplies a numerical reward already provided by another card. The optimal construction is to include efficient defensive Battle modifiers and stack them.

### Obvious package construction

Patriotism points directly toward:

- Militias;
- Entrenchment;
- Fortifications;
- Stand Ground;
- Fealty;
- other advantage or +1 Battle cards;
- the Commandant's defensive Orders.

The result is a recognizable but prescribed defensive package rather than an open strategic vocabulary.

### No meaningful standalone identity

Its Battle effect is a generic defending advantage. Its Asset effect depends on other modifier cards. The card is therefore either routine by itself or a numerical multiplier in combination.

### Commandant lock

The card is strongly aligned with defending controlled Territory and provides little interesting use for the General. Its function does not transform between leaders.

### False excitement

Doubling advantage may produce a large number, but it does not produce a new board decision, timing dilemma, or operational story. It risks making important battles less interactive rather than more memorable.

### Name and theme are not distinctly Military enough to justify preservation

Patriotism could belong to political, diplomatic, revolutionary, or ideological designs as easily as to Military. The name does not rescue the mechanical concept.

## Rubric result

- **New decision:** Fail.
- **Faction behavior:** Fail beyond generic defense.
- **Interaction breadth:** Numerically broad but strategically shallow.
- **Standalone floor:** Generic.
- **Leader interpretation:** Commandant lock.
- **Mandatory-engine risk:** High within the obvious defensive-modifier package.
- **Counterplay:** Mostly cancellation or avoiding the battle.
- **Memorable potential:** Low.
- **Obsolescence risk:** Makes smaller defensive effects into inputs for one multiplier and can distort their pricing.
- **Meaningful tension:** Absent.

## Recommendation

**Remove the current Patriotism design from Military consideration.**

No mechanical element needs to carry forward. The name may remain available for a future concept elsewhere, but it should not constrain the Military package.

**Release-slot status:** Unfilled.

# 4. Shock and Awe

## Current direction

Shock and Awe is a cost-5 all-in operation. The current v0.6 direction uses a prepared Asset and, after winning an initiated battle on an enemy-controlled Territory, grants immediate capture plus one follow-up advance. It consumes all Command after resolving any Command gained from that victory.

## What works

### Strong statement-card fantasy

The name promises a major offensive operation and is appropriately ambitious for a cost-5 Military card.

### Visible preparation and commitment

A prepared Asset gives the opponent warning and competes for Asset-bank capacity. Exhausting Command creates a real resource cost.

### Memorable moment

A decisive operation that changes the board and may continue forward can produce exactly the kind of story a premium faction card should create.

### Strong connection to Military's core game

The card engages directly with battle, enemy-controlled Territory, capture, movement, and breakthrough rather than creating a disconnected minigame.

## What fails

### It grants both sides of Military's central tension

The current effect provides immediate consolidation through capture **and** continued momentum through follow-up movement. The player does not choose between securing the gain and pressing onward.

### Severe overlap

The card overlaps with:

- Neutral Assimilation's immediate capture;
- the General's Rout follow-up movement;
- the Commandant's Fortify immediate capture;
- Neutral Invasion's offensive movement and battle support.

A cost-5 faction statement card should not read as several existing effects bundled together.

### Strong General skew

The trigger requires a battle the Military player initiated, and the follow-up movement is naturally offensive. The Commandant can use it, but it does not meaningfully express the Commandant's defend-counterattack-consolidate identity.

### Win-more and snowball risk

The card requires winning on enemy-controlled ground, then converts that success into a captured Territory and another advance. This can remove the ordinary counterattack window and compound an already favorable result.

### Counterplay is concentrated before resolution

Once the battle is won, the opponent loses both the normal capture-delay opportunity and potentially another board position. The visible Asset helps, but the payoff may still be too automatic and complete.

## Rubric result

- **New decision:** Partial. Preparation and commitment are good; the payoff itself lacks a momentum-versus-consolidation choice.
- **Faction behavior:** Strong.
- **Interaction breadth:** Strong, but with substantial duplication.
- **Standalone floor:** Narrow but appropriate for a capstone.
- **Leader interpretation:** General-heavy.
- **Mandatory-engine risk:** Low as a narrow cost-5, but it could become the default Military capstone if too complete.
- **Counterplay:** Visible preparation, but insufficient after the battle result.
- **Memorable potential:** Excellent.
- **Obsolescence risk:** High relative to Assimilation, Rout, Fortify, and other premium offensive tools.
- **Meaningful tension:** Strong in setup; weak in resolution.

## Recommendation

**Preserve Shock and Awe as the leading cost-5 Military statement-card concept, but rebuild it from the ground up.**

The redesign should:

- preserve visible preparation and serious commitment;
- make the player choose between momentum and consolidation, or otherwise prevent receiving every premium outcome at once;
- avoid merely bundling Assimilation, Rout, and Fortify;
- provide meaningful use or interpretation for both leaders;
- retain overextension risk;
- leave the opponent with anticipatory, positional, or recovery counterplay;
- feel capable of defining a game without becoming required for Military to function.

Cost 5 is thematically and structurally appropriate for the redesign, but no exact effect is approved.

**Release-slot status:** Unfilled until a redesigned card passes review.

---

# Package implications

The inherited list currently contributes:

- **two viable design seeds:** Brothers in Arms and Shock and Awe;
- **zero approved exact-text cards;**
- **zero occupied release slots;**
- **two effects recommended for retirement:** Militias and Patriotism.

The next Military design work should not begin by filling eight vacancies around four inherited cards. It should begin with twelve open slots, using the two surviving seeds only when they produce stronger cards than new alternatives.

Recommended next sequence:

1. decide whether these four audit recommendations are approved;
2. if approved, update the active card registry and Military notes to reflect candidate retirement and redesign status;
3. redesign Brothers in Arms at the concept level;
4. redesign Shock and Awe as a cost-5 statement card;
5. identify the remaining strategic decisions missing from the package before drafting additional cards.
